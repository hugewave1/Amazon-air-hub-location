The Business Question

Hub location is a half-billion-dollar bet.

Amazon Air operates a primary sortation hub at Cincinnati (CVG) and three regional hubs at San Bernardino, Lakeland, and Wilmington Ohio. As the network grows, the wrong next-hub choice costs hundreds of millions in fixed investment and decades of operating inefficiency. The right one compresses last-mile distance for hundreds of millions of packages annually.

Amazon's specific selection criteria are proprietary. But the underlying inputs — air-cargo flow, airport infrastructure, population density, weather, and network geometry — are all in public data. Recreating this decision from public data is exactly the kind of analytical exercise a network strategy team would actually run.

The Approach

One index. Five dimensions. Real-world validation.

Every candidate airport receives a 0–100 score on each of five dimensions, blended into a Hub Fitness Index using weights that are stress-tested through sensitivity analysis.

📦

Cargo activity

2025 freight throughput from BTS T-100 Segment data, log-scaled to handle a 700× range.

🛬

Runway capacity

Longest runway plus number of long runways from FAA Airport Master Record.

👥

Population catchment

People within a 4-hour drive proxy from US Census + TIGER county polygons.

☁️

Weather reliability

Placeholder latitude proxy pending NOAA disruption-day data integration.

🗺️

Geographic complementarity

Distance to nearest existing Amazon hub — rewards filling network gaps, not duplicating coverage.

Finding 1 · Cargo Concentration

US air cargo is wildly concentrated.

The top 20 airports handle 72% of all US domestic cargo. The top 3 alone — SDF (UPS), MEM (FedEx), and ORD — handle a third of the country's freight. This is a hub-and-spoke economy with very few hubs.

Finding 2 · The Existing Network

Amazon's Midwest belt is dense. The interior West is wide open.

Amazon Air's 28 facilities cluster along an SDF–CVG–IND–CMH–ILN axis in the Midwest, plus coastal anchors. Large swaths — the Mountain West, Pacific Northwest beyond Seattle, the Upper Midwest beyond Minneapolis — have no Amazon Air presence.

The Recommendation

Two practical hubs. One revealing artifact.

After scoring all 80 top-cargo candidates across all five dimensions, three airports lead the pack. Two — JFK and DFW — are real recommendations. The third — HNL — is where the model breaks in a useful way.

Why HNL surfaces — and why it's not the recommendation

Honolulu scores #1 because it's perfectly isolated from every existing Amazon hub (2,621 mi) and has favorable weather under the latitude proxy. But its 0.4M population catchment and offshore position mean it cannot serve mainland delivery routes — Anchorage already functions as Amazon's Pacific gateway. This is a useful methodological boundary case, not a business recommendation. The practical top 2 are JFK and DFW.

Honest Limitations

What this analysis doesn't capture.

A real-world hub investment decision would not be made on this analysis alone. Five named limitations — each with a specific remediation path.

Weather is a placeholder

Uses latitude as a proxy. Real NOAA disruption-day data would refine all weather scores and likely drop HNL substantially.

Straight-line catchment distance

240-mile radius is a 4-hour drive proxy. Real isochrones via routing APIs (OSRM) would refine catchment for top picks.

No cost dimension

Real estate, labor wages, and cost-of-living matter. BLS QCEW + COLI integration would penalize JFK and favor DFW.

Hand-built facility list

Amazon doesn't publish a comprehensive facility list. Best-effort compilation from press releases and FAA filings.

Weights are subjective

Default 20%-each is a neutral starting point. Real decisions calibrate weights to stated network strategy.
